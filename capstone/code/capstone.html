Your Character Has Been Created

Topic 1: Sexualization of Women in Video Games

Women in video games are generally far more sexualized than men are. They wear very revealing clothing compared to the men and are portrayed in much more sexual ways. This sexualization of women is not only limited to the games themselves, but also to their advertisements, covers, and online reviews.

In a content analysis performed by B. Beasley and T.C. Standley, a significant sex bias was found in the the way in which the male and female characters were dressed. The authors studied gender representations in 47 randomly selected games associated with the Nintendo 64 and Sony PlayStation consoles, coding each character that appeared in the first 20 minutes of playing each game and noting their clothing. They found that female characters wore less clothing and exposed more skin than male characters. Sleeve length was compared with gender in a cross-tabulation, and the result of this showed that nearly one half of the female characters were shown with no sleeves. Most of the women were wearing clothing such as halter tops, tank tops, and bathing suits. A cross-tabulation of neckline showed similar results. Of those characters shown with a low neckline, in which cleavage or pecs were visible, 85.71% were women. When cleavage was featured for a female character, 40.9% of the women were coded as voluptuous, 56.3% as average in breast size, and only 2.8% as “flat” chested. Overall, Beasley and Standley find strong evidence of gender stereotyping in the games, with females presented largely as sexual objects.


Beasley, B. & Standley, T. C. (2009). Shirts vs. Skins: Clothing as an Indicator of Gender Role Stereotyping in Video Games. Mass Communication and Society, 5(3), 279-293

A 2009 content analysis by J. Ivory used video game reviews from a heavily trafficked Internet site to investigate the prevalence and portrayal of male and female video game characters. The coding scheme recorded the gender of a character by the gender-related characteristics or the gender-specific pronouns that the reviewer used, and also measured the sexual attractiveness of that character by observing how they were described in the reviewers' commentary, whether tersely (e.g., “dashing,” “beautiful”) or in detail (e.g., “looks sexy in that revealing outfit”). The results showed that female characters were proportionally more often sexualized in comparison to their male counterparts. Though female characters were underrepresented overall, their attractiveness and sexuality were mentioned in proportionally more reviews than that of males. Less than 1% of the 75 reviews mentioning male characters included a reference to a male character’s attractiveness or sexual appeal, but 12% of the 42 reviews mentioning female characters included such references to females.


Ivory, J. (2009). Still a Man’s Game: Gender Representation in Online Reviews of Video Games. Mass communication & society, 9(1). doi:10.1207/s15327825mcs0901_6

Burgess and Stermer (2007) conducted a content analysis that analyzed 225 console video game covers obtained from online retail sites for portrayals of men and women. Every character on the covers was coded as either human or non-human, and further coded by gender (male, female, or unknown). Additionally, each human character was further coded as a whole figure or part of a figure. The study also measured physical objectification for both genders using metrics such as being portrayed in a sexy way, exaggerated bust/groin, vulgar clothing, tight clothing to accentuate body parts, revealing dress and/or being portrayed as simply a part other than a face (e.g., just thighs). They found that female characters were far more likely to be objectified than the males. 84% of female characters (38/45) were depicted as simply a body part, compared to 55% of male characters. Physical objectification of females occurred on 47.4% of the covers and 42.3% of the characters were portrayed in physically objectified ways. For males, physical objectification occurred in 13.5% of the covers and 5.8% of the characters were portrayed in physically objectified ways.


Burgess, M., Stermer, S., & Burgess, S. (2007). Sex, Lies, and Video Games: The Portrayal of Male and Female Characters on Video Game Covers. Sex roles., 57(5-6).

Scharrer (2009) conducted a content analysis of 1,054 video game advertisements contained in large-circulation video game magazines. Though the study's main focus is depictions of violence, it also examines the portrayal of gender and the extent of sexualization in the images and text of the ads. Results show that when female characters were present in the ads, they are largely shown in a way that is consistent with male fantasies—that is, as highly attractive and highly sexualized. In the ads examined, females wore tight-fitting clothing, showed cleavage or otherwise had their breasts emphasized, and were very commonly voluptuous and thin.


Scharrer, E. (2009). Virtual Violence: Gender and Aggression in Video Game Advertisements. Mass Communication and Society, 7(4), 393-412

A. Summers and M. Miller(2014) examined video game magazines and analyzed the portrayal of female video game characters and how this portrayal has changed in the last twenty years. The video game magazine articles coded ranged from 1988 - 2007. Coders were instructed to read the articles and rate the female characters on various scales, such as "sexy," "helpless," and "innocent." They were also instructed to rate how revealing the character's attire is. A total of 223 female characters from 175 game magazines were coded. The results of the study found that, over time, there has been a decrease in benevolent sexism and an increase in hostile sexism. Benevolent sexism includes traditional gender role attitudes for women, a sense of protective paternalism, a desire for intimacy, and complementary gender differentiation where women are perceived as more moral or pure than men. Hostile sexism endorses the exploitation of women as sexual objects and the idea of dominant paternalism, where men have the power and women do not. In other words, over the past 20 years, there have been less damsals in distress, but more sexualized women with revealing outfits.


A. Summers & M. K. Miller (2014). From Damsels in Distress to Sexy Superheroes. Feminist Media Studies, 14(6), 1028-1040.

E. Behm-Morawitz(2014) conducted a content analysis to examine the diversity of gender and race representations in video game advertising in the USA. The study coded 383 US magazine advertisements of console, mobile, and PC video games. Behm-Morawitz pays special attention to the ways race intersects with gender in character representations in video game advertisements. Her study examines how the representations of race/ethnicity and gender are tied to character appearance (e.g., provocativeness of attire) and depicted behaviors (e.g., acting violently). Coders judged, among other things, the gender, apparent race, violent behavior, attractiveness, body type, and how revealing the attire is of up to six prominant characters from each ad. The results found that female characters are disproportionately portrayed as attractive in comparison to male characters, especially White and Asian female characters. Additionally, female characters are significantly more likely than male characters to have a sexualized appearance, especially white female characters. Finally, the results indicate that females were disproportionately likely to be portrayed in various states of undress, with a greater degree of skin showing than their male counterparts.


Behm-Morawitz, E. (2014). Examining the intersection of race and gender in video game advertising. Journal of Marketing Communications, 23(17), 220-239

Topic 2: Lack of Significant Women in Video Games

Typically, women have much less representation in video games than men do. Many games have fewer female characters than men. Furthermore, women are shown less than men, but when they are shown, they are depicted in much more sexualized ways, which also hurts their representation. Additionally, fewer women play and develop video games, which further limits the chances for representation.

D. Williams, N. Martins, M. Consalvo, and J. Ivory conducted a large-scale content analysis in 2009 that with the objective of breaking down the representations of gender, race, and age in comparison to the general population. The sample included 150 games from a year across nine platforms, with the results weighted according to game sales to focus on the games that were actually being played by the public. There were a total of 8572 characters coded. Finally, to gain comparative baseline data from the actual US population, US census data was collected from the government census website. The results show that male characters are vastly more likely to appear than female characters. Compared to the 50.9/49.1 percent distribution in the actual population, the overall difference between men and women in video games is much more imbalanced at 85.23/14.77.


Williams, D., Martins, N., Consalvo, M., & Ivory, J. (2009). The virtual census: representations of gender, race and age in video games. New media & society., 11(5). doi:10.1177/1461444809105354

In 2014, X. Kondrat used survey research to gauge how gamers feel about the current representation of women in video games. The method used was an anonymous questionnaire that was sent out through social media so it was impossible to predict the amount of female and male respondents. About 78% of the respondents were male, and the most predominant age group was from 18-25 years old, showing that the video game audience is primarily male. The questionnaire asked both quantitative closed-end questions and qualitative open-ended questions about the respondent's opinion on the representation of females in video games. Results show that there is prevalent negative stereotyping and underrepresentation of the female gender. Notably, the answers show that the target audience of video games desires improvements in presentation of female gender as well as male. Additionally, Kondrat also privately emailed two Game Design experts (U. Benjaminsson and A. Mayes) and interviewed them. Both experts agreed that there are a lack of professionals in the game industry who are aware of gender studies and are qualified in them, which limits the quality of representation in the medium.


Kondrat, X. (2015). Gender and video games: How is female gender generally represented in various genres of video games? Journal of comparative research in anthropology and sociology., 6(1), 171-193.

J. Ivory's 2009 content analysis also delves into the lack of female characters in video games. The coding scheme recorded the gender of a character by the gender-related characteristics or the gender-specific pronouns that the reviewer used. It also included the activity/passivity of a character by judging their presence in the reviewers' commentary. The results showed that female characters were represented in far fewer of the sampled game reviews than male characters. Though 75% of the reviews mentioned male characters, only 42% mentioned female characters in any capacity. Images including male characters appeared in 78% of the reviews, whereas female characters appeared in images accompanying only 32% of the reviews.


Ivory, J. (2009). Still a Man’s Game: Gender Representation in Online Reviews of Video Games. Mass communication & society, 9(1). doi:10.1207/s15327825mcs0901_6

Burgess and Stermer (2007)'s' content analysis of console video game covers also sheds light on the scarcity of female characters in video game advertisements. Every character on the covers was coded as either human or non-human, and further coded by gender (male, female, or unknown) and apparant relevance to the game (primary vs. ancillary). They found that male characters were significantly more likely to be present overall on the covers of video games. 90.2% of the covers with humans portrayed men and 42.7% portrayed women. In terms of medians and ranges: Male characters ranged in frequency from 0 to 20 per cover while females ranged from zero to five. The median number of males portrayed per cover was one, while the median number of females portrayed was zero.


Burgess, M., Stermer, S., & Burgess, S. (2007). Sex, Lies, and Video Games: The Portrayal of Male and Female Characters on Video Game Covers. Sex roles., 57(5-6).

B. Beasley and T.C. Standley's 2009 content analysis also shows a significant sex bias was found in the number of male versus female characters found in the games. The authors studied gender representations in 47 randomly selected games associated with the Nintendo 64 and Sony PlayStation consoles, coding each character that appeared in the first 20 minutes of playing each game. Each game was also coded by rating, ranging from E (for everyone) to M (for mature audiences). Of the 597 characters coded, 71.5% were men, 13.7% were women, and 14.7% were undeterminable. Furthermore, the results show that female characters are equally poorly represented in all ratings of games.


Beasley, B. & Standley, T. C. (2009). Shirts vs. Skins: Clothing as an Indicator of Gender Role Stereotyping in Video Games. Mass Communication and Society, 5(3), 279-293

Dietz (1998) used content analysis to examine the portrayal of women and the use of violent themes in a sample of 33 popular Nintendo and Sega Genesis video games. Each of the games were coded into the following categories: no female characters at all, female characters portrayed as sex objects or prizes, females as the victim, females as the hero, and females in feminine roles. The results showed that the most common portrayal of women was actually the complete absence of women at all. 41% of the games coded had no primary female characters, and 30% of the games coded did not represent the female population at all.


Dietz, T. L. (1998). An Examination of Violence and Gender Role Portrayals in Video Games: Implications for Gender Socialization and Aggressive Behavior. Sex Roles, 38(5-6), 425-442

Topic 3: Passiveness of Women in Video Games

Women in video games tend to see far less action than men do. Player-controlled female characters are rarer than player-controlled male characters, and relatively few video games have female main characters as opposed to male. Women are often depicted as passive "damsals in distress" who wait for the men to rescue them.

Burgess and Stermer's (2007) content analysis of console video game covers is again relevant for discussions of the prominance of female characters vs. male characters. Every character on the covers was coded as either human or non-human, and further coded by gender (male, female, or unknown) and apparant relevance to the game (primary vs. ancillary). The study also measured role objectification for both genders using metrics such as being portrayed without action, being in a submissive role, being a reward to others, appearing unnecessary to the goal of the game, and requiring rescue without any clear attempts to help oneself. Among the ancillary characters, they found that the amount of men and women who were role objectified were roughly equal. However, among the primary characters, 20% of the female characters are role objectified compared to absolutely none of the primary male characters. Furthermore, they found a clear difference in how active and essential female and male characters are to the game. Male characters are almost five times more likely than female characters to be portrayed as the primary characters. Furthermore, of the primary female characters, over 30% of those were accompanied by a primary male character. By contrast, only 13% of primary male characters were accompanied by a primary female.


Burgess, M., Stermer, S., & Burgess, S. (2007). Sex, Lies, and Video Games: The Portrayal of Male and Female Characters on Video Game Covers. Sex roles., 57(5-6).

Williams, Martins, Consalvo, and Ivory's virtual census of 2009 is helpful in breaking down the relevance of female characters and male characters. The sample included 150 games from a year across nine platforms, with the results weighted according to game sales to focus on the games that were actually being played by the public. There were a total of 8572 characters coded. Each character was coded for their status as a primary (player-controlled) or a secondary (computer-controlled) character. As stated before, the difference between male and female characters in video games was found to be much larger than the difference in real life, according to US census data. Among primary "action" characters, males are even more likely to appear and the difference is even larger. Thus, males appear more frequently in games than females, and even more so as drivers of the action. When females do appear, they are more likely to be in secondary roles than primary ones.


Williams, D., Martins, N., Consalvo, M., & Ivory, J. (2009). The virtual census: representations of gender, race and age in video games. New media & society., 11(5). doi:10.1177/1461444809105354

J. Ivory's 2009 content analysis of video game reviews from a heavily trafficked Internet site also investigated the prevalence of male and female video game characters. The coding scheme recorded the gender of a character by the gender-related characteristics or the gender-specific pronouns that the reviewer used. It also included the activity/passivity of a character by judging their presence in the reviewers' commentary. The results showed that female characters were represented in far fewer of the sampled game reviews than male characters. Furthermore, only a small portion of reviews that indicated primary characters named a female lead (10.9%) or primary characters of both genders (13.0%). Comparison of male and female characters’ appearance in active and passive roles found similar differences. Active male characters were mentioned in 75% of the reviews, whereas active female characters were described in only 33% of the reviews. Also, 65% of the reviews indicated male playable characters, whereas only 22% mentioned female playable characters.


Ivory, J. (2009). Still a Man’s Game: Gender Representation in Online Reviews of Video Games. Mass communication & society, 9(1). doi:10.1207/s15327825mcs0901_6

The anonymous questionnaire was sent out through social media so it was impossible to predict the amount of female and male respondents. About 78% of the respondents were male, and the most predominant age group was from 18-25 years old. The results showed that much of the video game audience agrees with the statement that female characters are rarely protagonists of video games, although they do believe it has improved in recent years.

Topic 4: Stereotyping of Women in Video Games

Women in video games are often subjected to stereotypes. Some of which include the previously discussed archetypes of the "damsal in distress" or the overly sexualized woman. However, these stereotypes can have powerful effects on how players of video games perceive about real-world women. Furthermore, the stereotyping issues in video games do not limit themselves to women; racial minorities and even men are also stereotyped.

X. Kondrat(2014)'s survey research is mainly about the stereotyping of women in video games. The results of the study show that there is prevalent negative stereotyping of the female gender. Based on the replies of the respondents of the survey the most popular ways of stereotyping females in video games is by creating female characters which are sexually provocative and are almost undressed or dressed in a seductive way. Also, there is a lack of female protagonist in video games which can represent female as strong and independent. Most of the respondents agreed that female gender stereotyping in video games happens because of the predominant male target audience. Several female respondents reported having negative perceptions of themselves due to the unrealistic sexualization of women in video games. Respondents also expressed concern about the negative stereotyping of men as well as women. Additionally, Kondrat also privately emailed two Game Design experts (U. Benjaminsson and A. Mayes) and interviewed them. The experts agreed that there was a "vicious circle" where "there are not enough developers creating strong women, so publishers and developers don’t think that games with women sell, so they don’t make games with strong women” (Mayes, 2014). This may explain why the problem of stereotyping is so prevalent in the medium.


Kondrat, X. (2015). Gender and video games: How is female gender generally represented in various genres of video games? Journal of comparative research in anthropology and sociology., 6(1), 171-193.

A. Summers and M. Miller's 2014 examination of video game magazines found that the portrayal of female video game characters had changed in the last twenty years from one stereotype into another. A total of 223 female characters from 175 game magazines were coded. The results of the study found that, over time, there has been a decrease in benevolent sexism and an increase in hostile sexism. In the discussion of their results, Summers and Miller note that this sexist shift may impact attitudes, which have very real consequences outside the game arena. Specifically, these portrayals of women may impact how males view women. They cite several studies that show that portraying women as sex objects may encourage negative attitudes toward women. Furthermore, they are also concerned with the negative implications for how women view themselves. They again cite sources that state that women who are exposed to sexist ads end up having lower self-esteem. They conclude that the stereotypes caused by both benevolent and hostile sexism can lead youths to adopt these sexist attitudes, which can be detrimental to their perception of the world.


Alicia S. & Monica K. M. (2014). From Damsels in Distress to Sexy Superheroes. Feminist Media Studies, 14(6), 1028-1040.

Scharrer's 2009 content analysis of video game advertisements contained in large-circulation video game magazines also shows the harmful stereotypes that arise from the depiction of women in video games. Scharrer discusses two specific psychological concepts in her research: hypermasculinity and hyperfemininity. Hypermasculitiny is a term applied to performances of masculinity in which “macho” characteristics are carried out to the point of exaggeration. Traits of HM include callous attitudes towards sex, perceiving violence as manly, and perceiving danger as thrilling. Hyperfemininity is a term used to describe individuals’ adherence to a stereotypical and exaggerated feminine gender role idealization that emphasizes dependence, passivity, and a view of women as sexual objects. The core traits of HF include the belief that a woman's success depends on acquiring a man, the use of sexuality to secure a relationship, and the expectation that men meet traditional gender roles that include aggression and the initiation of sex. Prior to her research, Scharrer hypothesized that males would appeal to notions of HM and females would take roles that emphasize HF. The results did show evidence for HM, especially the pursuit of danger as thrill. Evidence for HF was also present, but not as present as HM because female characters were not emphasized or developed enough in the ad to be able to assign some motive for their behavior. Overall, the results of this content analysis demonstrate rather convincingly that gender stereotypes abound in video games.


Scharrer, E. (2009). Virtual Violence: Gender and Aggression in Video Game Advertisements. Mass Communication and Society, 7(4), 393-412

E. Behm-Morawitz's 2014 content analysis examines the diversity of gender and race representations in video game advertising in the USA, and delves into the harmful stereotypes these advertisements portray. The study coded 383 US magazine advertisements of console, mobile, and PC video games. As discussed above, the results found that female characters are disproportionately sexualized in comparison to male characters. However, it is notable that White and Asian female characters are especially so, and Black female characters are not sexualized to the same degree. The study also finds that Black men are portrayed as the most muscular, but also the least attractive, as well as the most likely to wield a fun. Asian characters were the most violent and the most likely to wield swords. And White characters are depicted as the least violent and most attractive group. Overall, the study concludes that video game advertisements uphold some longstanding media stereotypes of racial/ethnic minorities and women. Support can be found for the stereotypic portrayals of the White male hero, submissive sexualized female, Asian ninja, and deviant Black male. Behm-Morawitz argues that video game advertisers’ reliance on stereotypes to appeal to a mass audience has an impact beyond sales, and cites Scharrer to argue that video game advertisements highlight prominent game representations and reflect the cultural messages about race/ ethnicity, gender, and violence being communicated to video game fans.


Behm-Morawitz, E. (2014). Examining the intersection of race and gender in video game advertising. Journal of Marketing Communications, 23(17), 220-239

Topic 5: Where do we go from here?

By now, we have seen enough evidence to conclude that women have an overall negative representation in video games at this point in time. Female characters in video games are subjected to overexcessive sexualization, a lack of significant representation, a lack of active and strong characters, and prolific stereotyping. However, there is still hope for improvement. So what lies in the future of the representation of women in video games?

X. Kondrat(2014)'s survey research is evidence that improvements may come in the future. Based on the replies of the respondents, the audience of video games are also concerned about the negative stereotyping of women as well as men. The majority of them have also expressed a desire for changes. Several respondents also proposed solutions of their own through the open-ended questions, which included introducing more female game developers to the industry, attracting more females to play video games, and creating more female protagonists of video games who are not objectified or sexualized. Additionally, many also reported that the situation has been improving in recent years and that they have hope for the future. According to the responses of the two experts that Kondrat interviewed, there has been a steady improvement in the representation of the female gender in alternative and independent video game companies. However, these games, despite having good female representation, are not being played because they are made by smaller companies. The large mainstream game companies continue to create games using the stereotypes and show little signs of change. One expert, Benjaminsson, comments that the companies are only after money and they want to develop what will sell, so our best chance at this point is "an educated and critical audience to reward and appreciate challenging and thoughtful games.” By showing the companies that there is indeed a market for games with good female representation, we may be able to change their ways.


Kondrat, X. (2015). Gender and video games: How is female gender generally represented in various genres of video games? Journal of comparative research in anthropology and sociology., 6(1), 171-193.

A. Summers and M. Miller's 2014 examination of video game magazines tracked the changes in the portrayal of female video game characters in the last twenty years. The results of the study found that, over time, there has been a decrease in benevolent sexism and an increase in hostile sexism. Contrasting Summers and Miller's findings with Dietz's from 16 years earlier illustrates the transition very clearly. Dietz's study did not find an excessive amount of sexualization; only 28% of the games were found to have extremely sexualized women. In its stead, Dietz was concerned with the recurring depiction of women as "damsals in distress" who need rescuing from the main male character. On the other hand, Summers and Miller found few dainty feminine princesses who require rescue. Instead, they found sexy and sexualized females who could be the active protagonist of their game. This progression shows that the current state of female representation in video is not fixed, but malleable. If it changed once before, surely it could change again. Although we are still not at an ideal place right now, having simply transitioned from one form of sexism to another, the fact that we have improved upon the issues that were prolific in Dietz's day shows that there is a chance that we will resolve today's issues as well.

A. Harvey, in her 2014 paper "Everyone Can Make Games!", analyzes the recent calls for diversity and inclusion of female developers in the industry of digital games. According to Harvey, the digital game industry is currently in a transition period of feminism and postfeminism. As such, there is significant tension between women-focused inclusivity movements such as the Difference Engine Initiative (DEI) and post-feminists. In particular, she focuses on three post-feminist articulations by notable female figures in the North American digital games community to provide insight into the process of bringing change into a conservative culture of production. The first is Robin Hunicke’s 2010 Indie Game Rant at the Game Developers Conference, which was the inspiration for the DEI. The second is an article written in October 2011 by games journalist Leigh Alexander entitled "I’m Tired of Being a ‘Woman in Games.’ I’m a Person." Finally, the third example is a 2012 presentation at the Game Developers Conference (GDC) by independent game designer Mare Sheppard entitled “Why I Hate Women in Games Initiatives.” These three pieces all illustrate the contradictions and difficulty in being a spokesperson for gender issues as a female in a context that is deeply unfriendly to women in general, and decidedly hostile towards feminists in particular. Hunicke and Alexander both lament their status as the "token woman," where they must constantly pay lip-service to the importance of diversity and the need to address and rectify the rampant sexism in the industry. Sheppard criticizes women-only initiatives like the DEI and equates them to a "victim support group" that only serves to discriminate against interested participants. She instead calls for systems of meritocracy where one's gender does not any advantages or disadvantages. Harvey's analyses show that the game industry is in a turbulent stage in recent years, and there are many directions in which it could go. However, whether it be through the feminist method of inclusionary initiatives like the DEI or post-feminist meritocratic methods, there will someday be sufficient opportunities for women to enter the currently male-dominated industry of digital games. And with more female developers will likely come better female characters and better female representation.


Harvey, A. & Fisher, S. (2014). “Everyone Can Make Games!”: The post-feminist context of women in digital game production. Feminist Media Studies, 15(4), 576-592.
